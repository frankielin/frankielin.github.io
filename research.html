This Just In: Unemployment Surprises and Their Transmission Through the TV News
with Christopher Tice-Raskin
[+abstract]
This paper examines how local exposure to economic news on television influences labor market outcomes in U.S. media markets. To do this, we construct a novel measure of exposure to unemployment news by interacting market‑level television viewership shares with national unemployment forecast errors. We address potential endogeneity in television viewing patterns by using lagged viewership shares, leveraging the high persistence of television viewership rates. The results indicate that higher exposure to news of higher‑than‑expected unemployment reduces local unemployment rates. Additional analysis shows that greater exposure also reduces job destruction, increases separations, and raises hiring rates, consistent with models where workers with higher unemployment expectations engage in increased search intensity.

School Shootings and Academic Achievement: A Look at the 2014 Isla Vista Killings
Undergraduate Senior Thesis
[+abstract]
This study analyzes how one fatal shooting–the 2014 Isla Vista Killings at the University of California, Santa Barbara–affected various student groups, motivated by growing interest in how school-related gun violence impacts surviving students. Using detailed student-level data from several California universities, we estimate impacts at both the extensive margin, by comparing UCSB students to those at other universities using a synthetic control design, and at the intensive margin, by comparing students in victims' social networks to their UCSB peers using a standard difference-in-difference design. Observable outcomes include quarterly grades, course failure rates, and dropout rates. We find that the incident caused an immediate one-quarter grade increase along both margins, but UCSB students' academic performance deteriorated in subsequent quarters. Dropout rates for students in victims' social networks rose substantially, especially among female students.
